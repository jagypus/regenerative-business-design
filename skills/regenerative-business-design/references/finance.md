# Layer 5 — Finance & Capital Strategy

Source: Aunnie Patton Power (*Adventure Finance*, innovative.finance), plus steward-ownership finance practice (Purpose Ventures et al.) and Boyd's ergodicity framing.

Core idea: the grant↔VC-equity binary is a design failure. Between them lies a rich space of instruments. **Match the instrument to the venture's actual physics** — growth rate, margin structure, asset intensity, exit (non-)intention — instead of deforming the venture to fit the instrument. For a steward-owned/regenerative venture, conventional VC preferred equity is usually *structurally* unavailable (no exit, no control sale), so this layer is not optional.

## Diagnostic first: the venture's capital physics

Before naming instruments, establish:
1. **Growth shape** — blitzscale (rare), steady compounding, or capped-by-ecology (e.g., land-based businesses can't 10x a watershed)?
2. **Margin & cash curve** — when does it generate cash? How predictable are revenues?
3. **Asset intensity** — land, plant, inventory (financeable with asset-backed instruments) vs. pure opex?
4. **Exit intention** — none (steward-owned) / exit-to-purpose / exit-to-community? This kills or enables whole instrument classes.
5. **Risk profile & ruin lines** — what single events end the venture? (ergodicity check — capital structure should buy survival, not just growth)
6. **Who already has aligned money?** — customers, community, mission funders, aligned family offices often precede institutional capital.

## The instrument toolkit

### Dilution-free / revenue-linked
- **Revenue-based financing (RBF)**: investor receives a % of revenues until a cap (typically 1.3–3x principal). Fits: predictable revenues, decent margins, no-exit ventures. Watch: % of revenue strains low-margin businesses; cap and % must be modeled honestly.
- **Demand dividend**: RBF variant with payment holidays tied to cash-flow thresholds — payments flex with the venture's actual cash position. Pioneered in impact contexts (Santa Clara Univ. work); kind to seasonal/agricultural ventures.
- **SAFEs/convertibles with capped conversion → redeemable instruments**: in steward contexts, conversion to common equity is replaced by conversion to capped redeemable preferred or revenue share.

### Equity-like without control extraction
- **Redeemable equity**: shares the company buys back over time at a formula price (e.g., multiple of investment or book-value formula), giving investors equity-like upside with a built-in liquidity path that returns control. Fits steward ownership; precedent: many Purpose Ventures deals, Organically Grown's investor notes.
- **Non-voting / capped-dividend share classes**: FairShares investor class, golden-share-model investor shares. Returns capped and fair; control never transfers.
- **Patient/evergreen equity from aligned holders**: foundations' mission-related investments (MRIs/PRIs), steward-ownership funds (Purpose Ventures, and an expanding field — search current landscape when advising).

### Grant-adjacent & catalytic
- **Recoverable grants** (repay if milestones hit), **convertible grants** (convert to RBF/equity-like on success), **first-loss capital** (philanthropic tranche absorbing risk to crowd in commercial capital — blended finance).
- **Outcomes-based**: social/development impact bonds, environmental impact bonds, outcomes funds — where a government/funder pays for verified outcomes; relevant when the venture's regenerative outcomes (carbon, water quality, employment) have a payer.

### Community & ecological capital
- **Community investment**: crowdfunded community shares (UK co-op model), direct public offerings, customer prepayment/membership capital. Doubles as governance legitimacy (layer 4).
- **Ecological asset finance**: carbon/biodiversity credit prepurchases, water funds, habitat banking. Treat with integrity caution — verify additionality; don't let credit revenue redefine purpose (paradigm check: selling offsets is level 2–3; ecosystem regeneration that *also* monetizes residual credits can be level 4).

## Matching heuristics (defaults, not rules)

| Venture profile | Default capital stack |
|---|---|
| Steward-owned SaaS, steady growth | RBF or redeemable equity + founder-friendly working capital |
| Land-based regenerative ag/forestry | Patient asset-backed debt + recoverable grants + ecological outcome payments; community shares for the land via a land trust |
| Community enterprise (food, energy, care) | Community shares + blended philanthropic first-loss + cooperative bank debt |
| Deep-tech with regenerative mission | Catalytic grants + milestone-based convertible grants + aligned evergreen equity (steward conversion before scale-capital) |
| Conversion of existing company to steward ownership | Seller-financed sale to PPT/foundation repaid from future profits + steward-ownership funds + employee/community investment |

## Term-sheet integrity checklist

For any proposed financing, check:
- **Control**: does any term (board seats, protective provisions, drag-along, conversion triggers) create a path to purpose capture or forced sale? 
- **Cap & fairness**: are investor returns capped and is the cap *fair* given real risk (steward ownership argues for fair, not minimal, returns — underpaying capital is its own incoherence)?
- **Cash-flow realism**: model the payment obligations against conservative revenue scenarios; an RBF that starves the regenerative work is extraction wearing impact clothing.
- **Ruin protection**: do covenants/repayment terms create absorbing failure states in bad years? Prefer instruments that flex with reality (demand dividend logic).
- **Stakeholder symmetry**: who else is putting in value (sweat, land, trust) on what terms? Capital should not systematically out-rank contribution (FairShares principle).
- **Narrative honesty**: can you explain the deal to the community council in one page? If not, simplify.

## The honest downside ledger

Present these costs explicitly whenever recommending a purpose-locked capital strategy — founders deserve the comparison they'd get from a skeptical CFO, not a pitch:

- **Smaller capital pool.** Excluding conventional VC/PE removes the large majority of risk capital. The aligned pool (steward-ownership funds, foundations, community capital, values-aligned family offices) is growing but thin; raises take longer and often close smaller.
- **Slower, costlier raises.** Unfamiliar structures mean educating every investor and their counsel; legal drafting is bespoke (especially FairShares-style articles); diligence cycles stretch.
- **No conventional exit windfall — but founder upside is designable.** What's genuinely off the table is the sale-of-control jackpot (acquisition or IPO of voting stock). Substantial founder wealth can still be structured in: capped redeemable founder shares bought back at a formula price over time (the Organically Grown pattern, applied to founders), tiered profit participations or founder dividend rights that vest with tenure, phantom equity capped at a multiple of salary or investment, and seller-financed conversion payouts when an existing founder transitions a company into steward ownership. The honest framing for founders: upside arrives as cash flows over years from a durable company, capped at "wealthy", not "dynastic" — slower, more certain, and survivable for the company. Same logic for early employees: capped phantom/redeemable instruments replace standard options, which narrows the talent pool to the aligned — a feature *and* a real cost.
- **Less rescue capital.** In a bad year, a conventional company can sell itself or take dirty money; a purpose-locked one often can't. Survival planning (reserves, flexible instruments, demand-dividend logic) must substitute for the exit safety valve.
- **Instrument immaturity.** RBF/redeemable-equity case law, secondary markets, and benchmarks are thin compared to venture equity; pricing is artisanal and disputes have fewer precedents.
- **When conventional wins.** Be fair: if the venture genuinely needs blitzscale capital intensity, faces winner-take-all dynamics, or the founder's honest priority is personal wealth creation, a conventional structure with strong culture may serve better than a regenerative structure that starves. Saying so builds trust and is sometimes the right answer.

## Sequencing pattern

Typical regenerative venture sequence: founder + community capital → catalytic/philanthropic (recoverable grants, first-loss) → revenue-linked or redeemable instruments as cash flows mature → (if scale demands) aligned evergreen/institutional capital under locked structure. Raise each round *after* the ownership lock (layer 3) is in place — retrofitting steward ownership under a conventional cap table ranges from expensive to impossible.
