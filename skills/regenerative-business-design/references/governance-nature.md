# Layer 4 — Governance & the Voice of Nature

Ownership (layer 3) says who *holds* the company; governance says how decisions actually get made and **who is heard** — including stakeholders who cannot speak: ecosystems, future generations, the more-than-human world.

## Nature on the board

The headline move: give nature a formal seat in corporate governance, held by human guardians legally bound to represent its interests.

Precedents to cite (verify current status via web search when it matters):
- **Faith In Nature** (UK, 2022) — first company to appoint Nature to its board as a director, via a guardianship structure designed with Lawyers for Nature and Earth Law Center. Nature's vote is exercised by appointed guardians; directors' duties amended so the company's purpose includes nature's interests.
- **House of Hackney** (UK, 2023) — appointed both Nature and Future Generations as directors.
- **Patagonia** — "Earth is our only shareholder": ownership-level rather than board-level voice (see layer 3); the complementary pattern.
- Broader rights-of-nature context: Whanganui River legal personhood (NZ, 2017, Te Awa Tupua — guardianship by Māori iwi + Crown appointees), Ecuador's constitutional rights of nature, Mar Menor (Spain, 2022). These show guardianship-for-a-natural-entity is a tested legal pattern, not whimsy.

### Design questions for a nature directorship
1. **Which nature?** Abstract "Nature" or a specific ecosystem the business depends on (its watershed, sourcing landscapes)? Specific is stronger — it connects to the FBC's named biophysical stocks and gives guardians something assessable.
2. **Who guards?** Independent guardians with ecological standing (scientists, indigenous representatives, environmental lawyers); appointment and removal process insulated from management; a guardianship council rather than one individual reduces idiosyncrasy.
3. **What powers?** Spectrum: advisory voice → full directorship with vote → veto over defined "red-line" decisions (land use, sourcing changes, sale) → ownership-level golden share held by an ecological trust (strongest; bridges to layer 3).
4. **Accountable to what?** Give guardians an assessment basis: ecological health indicators from the FBC's outcomes blocks (soil organic matter, water quality, biodiversity indices), so "nature's interest" is evidence-based, not vibes.
5. **Legal mechanics** — typically: articles amended to include nature's interests in directors' duties + a guardianship agreement + (optionally) a share class or trust. Jurisdiction-dependent; companies-law duties to the company-as-a-whole can constrain how far a director may prefer nature; have counsel design around this.

## Future generations

Same guardianship logic, temporal instead of ecological: a director, council seat, or veto-holder charged with the interests of those affected 30–100+ years out. Precedents/inspirations: House of Hackney's Future Generations director; Wales's Well-being of Future Generations Act (a public-sector pattern adaptable to corporate charters); Japanese "Future Design" deliberation practice (a role-played future-generation perspective in decisions — cheap, surprisingly effective, good starter practice).

## Stakeholder voice architecture

Beyond nature, layer 4 distributes voice across the pentad (layer 1). Building blocks:

- **Stakeholder councils** — standing bodies for workers, suppliers, community, customers with defined rights: information, consultation, nomination of directors, or consent on defined matters. (FairShares Commons builds this into share classes; a council achieves a lighter version contractually.)
- **Sociocracy / dynamic governance** — consent-based decision-making in linked circles; useful as the *operating system* inside steward-owned companies so that distributed ownership doesn't degrade into either management capture or assembly paralysis. Key patterns: consent (no reasoned objection) not consensus; double-linked circles; regular role re-election.
- **Worker self-management patterns** — from Mondragon's social councils to Haier-style microenterprises; match the depth of democracy to the workforce's developmental readiness (Sanford: build capability, then authority — authority without capability discredits the model).
- **Reserved matters matrices** — the practical artifact: a table of decision types × voice-holders (who decides / who must consent / who must be consulted / who is informed). Producing this matrix is often the single most useful governance deliverable for a founder.

## Design method

1. From the FBC, list every stakeholder including ecological actors and future generations.
2. For each: what decisions most affect them? what voice do they currently have? what voice-form fits (information / consultation / consent / vote / veto / ownership)?
3. Draft the reserved-matters matrix.
4. Check coherence: governance promises that ownership structure (layer 3) can't protect will be broken under pressure — e.g., a nature director is decoration if a majority shareholder can remove the guardians or sell the company. Push protections down into the ownership layer where they must hold.
5. Start with the minimum living version: a guardianship agreement + reserved matters + one council beats an elaborate paper constitution nobody operates. Governance, like everything else here, develops.
