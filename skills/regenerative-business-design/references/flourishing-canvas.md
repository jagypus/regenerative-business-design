# Layer 2 — Value Creation Model

Two complementary instruments work this layer: the **Flourishing Business Canvas** (the map of what value is created and destroyed, for whom, across three contexts) and **Dembek's model families** (the venture's stance toward the system it touches: deliver, source, or reorganize). Draft the canvas; position the model; check the two against each other and against layer 1.

## Instrument 1: The Flourishing Business Canvas

Source: Antony Upward / the Strongly Sustainable Business Model Group (OCAD University, flourishingbusiness.org). The FBC extends Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas from one economic layer to three nested contexts: **environment** (the biosphere everything depends on), **society** (communities, culture, institutions), and **economy** (the conventional canvas, reframed).

A business model here answers: *how does this venture co-create flourishing — and honestly, what does it destroy — across all three contexts?*

## The canvas structure

Work through four perspectives and sixteen question-blocks. In conversation, don't march through all sixteen mechanically — cluster them, but make sure each gets answered before the canvas is "done".

### Perspective 1: PROCESS (how value is created)

- **Activities** — what the business actually does, including ecological and social processes it participates in (e.g., grazing patterns, community convening), not just commercial operations.
- **Resources** — what it uses: include natural stocks (water, soil, biomass) and social resources (trust, knowledge commons) alongside capital and equipment.
- **Biophysical stocks** — the natural reservoirs the business draws on or replenishes. Name them specifically: *this* aquifer, *this* forest. Are stocks growing or depleting because this business exists?
- **Ecosystem services** — the services nature provides the business free (pollination, water filtration, climate regulation). Making these visible is half the canvas's power: they are unpriced suppliers.
- **Partnerships** — formal and informal allies, including stewardship organizations, regulators, indigenous communities, research bodies.
- **Governance** — who gets to make which decisions (preview of layer 4; on the canvas, just capture current/intended decision rights and whose interests they serve).

### Perspective 2: VALUE (what is created and destroyed)

- **Value co-creations** — value delivered *with* (not just to) each stakeholder, in all three contexts: economic value, social value (health, capability, belonging), ecological value (habitat, carbon, soil).
- **Value co-destructions** — the honest block, and the one conventional canvases lack. What does this model degrade? Extraction, emissions, displaced livelihoods, attention costs, waste. Naming destructions is a design input, not a confession — each one is a redesign target or an honest residual.

### Perspective 3: PEOPLE (who is involved)

- **Stakeholders** — all of them, including non-customers affected by the model. **Include ecological actors as stakeholders** (the river, the pollinators, future generations) — assign them a voice-holder if possible (links to layer 4 guardianship).
- **Customers (needs)** — the human needs being met (use a needs framework like Max-Neef if helpful: subsistence, protection, participation, idleness, creation...). Distinguish needs from wants the model manufactures.
- **Relationships** — the quality and kind of relationship with each stakeholder group: transactional, communal, custodial?
- **Channels** — how value reaches stakeholders, with attention to channel externalities (e.g., last-mile emissions, platform dependencies).

### Perspective 4: OUTCOMES (how success is defined)

- **Goals** — tri-profit definition of success: the firm's chosen goals for environmental, social, AND economic outcomes. Not a single bottom line with two PR appendices.
- **Benefits** — the rewards generated, in all capitals (financial, natural, social, human), and who receives them. Christine McDougall's **synergistic accounting** practice (Syntropic World) is a useful discipline here: deliberately account for value that is relational, invisible, or unpriced — trust built, care given, ecological function restored — rather than letting "what we can measure" define "what counts".
- **Costs** — likewise in all capitals: who and what pays, including unpriced costs to ecosystems and communities. Honest cost accounting feeds the value co-destruction block.

## Facilitation guidance

- **Start with stakeholders and value, not activities.** Founders default to describing what they do. Pull them first to who is affected and what flourishing means for each — the activities then redesign themselves.
- **Make nature concrete.** "The environment" is a level-2 abstraction. Ask: which watershed? which species? which material flows? A named ecosystem can be a stakeholder; an abstraction cannot.
- **Use the destruction block early.** Asking "what does this destroy?" mid-process, when trust is established, unlocks more honesty than saving it for the end.
- **Tri-profit goals must be specific enough to fail.** "Improve soil health" is decoration; "raise soil organic matter on partner farms from 1.8% to 3% by 2030" is a goal. Push for falsifiability in all three contexts.
- **Canvas → coherence.** When the canvas is drafted, check it against layer 1 (does the model express the essence?) and forward to layers 3–5 (does anything in the model require ownership, governance, or capital that conventional structures would corrupt?).

## Common patterns worth knowing

- **Regenerative flywheels**: models where serving one context strengthens another (e.g., agroforestry: ecological restoration *is* the product pipeline). Look for these — they are the difference between a balanced scorecard and a living system.
- **Hidden dependency inversions**: the canvas often reveals that the business's most critical supplier is an unpriced ecosystem service. That discovery motivates layer 3–4 protections (e.g., giving the watershed a governance voice).
- **Multi-stakeholder value capture conflicts**: when two stakeholders' value claims collide (cheap product vs. living wages), don't average them away on the canvas — carry the tension into governance design.

## Instrument 2: Deliver, source, or reorganize — Dembek's model families

Source: Krzysztof (Chris) Dembek and colleagues. Key articles: Dembek, York & Singh, "Creating value for multiple stakeholders: Sustainable business models at the Base of the Pyramid" (*Journal of Cleaner Production*, 2018 — 55 organizations in Indonesia and the Philippines, yielding a nine-model matrix); York & Dembek, "Building BoP Business Models for Sustainable Poverty Alleviation: System Tips and System Traps" (2021); Dembek et al., "Untangling business model outcomes, impacts and value" (*Business Strategy and the Environment*, 2023). Developed studying poverty-alleviation ventures; the pattern generalizes to any venture addressing a degraded system.

Every business model takes one of three stances toward the community/system it touches:

1. **Delivering** — the system's people are *customers*: the model brings products or services to them. Value logic: meet needs.
2. **Sourcing** — the system's people and ecosystems are *suppliers*: the model buys from them. Value logic: create income and demand.
3. **Reorganizing** — the system itself is the *client*: the model restructures how the community and the systems around it operate (new market linkages, restored commons, rebuilt local capacity, changed flows of money, material, and power). Reorganizing models **combine all three value-creation logics** — they deliver, source, *and* rewire — which is why Dembek's research found they have the largest value-creation potential for complex, entangled problems.

### Why this matters for regenerative design

A level-4 regenerative ambition (layer 1) almost always implies a **reorganizing** model: "evolving the health of nested systems" is system-rewiring work, not product delivery. So run the stance check as a coherence test: *a regenerative essence statement sitting on a pure delivering model is the layer-2 version of the purpose-washing incoherence* — the purpose says "regenerate the watershed," the model says "sell things to people who live near it." Either deepen the model toward reorganizing or honestly scale back the stated ambition.

The reorganizing stance is also where the FBC's **regenerative flywheels** live: when the model's commercial activity *is* the system-restructuring activity (agroforestry, community-owned energy, fibersheds), delivering and sourcing become spokes of the reorganizing wheel.

### System traps — the honest ledger per family

Each family has a characteristic failure mode (York & Dembek's "system traps"). Name the trap for whichever family the venture sits in:

- **Delivering trap**: scaling consumption. Growth of the model = growth of throughput; without ecological design from inception, poverty alleviation or customer benefit gets tied to environmental degradation.
- **Sourcing trap**: extraction in fair-trade clothing. Cost pressure pushes the model to squeeze suppliers or ecosystems; the sourcing relationship reproduces the dependency it claimed to fix.
- **Reorganizing trap**: lock-in. Rewiring a community's systems can lock it into the *wrong* structure or technology — the model's very success entrenches an arrangement that later proves unsustainable. Reorganizers carry the largest responsibility precisely because they reach deepest.

Corresponding design disciplines ("system tips"): embed ecological considerations from inception, not retrofit; co-design with the community rather than for it (the affected system must author its own reorganization — this is Sanford's indirect work in business-model form); think in system interconnections, not single value chains; prefer long-term system health over rapid scaling.

### Outputs ≠ outcomes ≠ impacts

From Dembek's later work (2023): a model's *outputs* (what it produces), *outcomes* (changes for stakeholders), and *impacts* (durable changes in the system) are distinct links in a causal chain, each produced by identifiable mechanisms. Claiming impact from outputs is the layer-2 form of greenwash. When drafting tri-profit goals on the canvas, write the mechanism chain explicitly: activity → output → outcome → system impact — and put the falsifiable target at the *impact* end where possible.

### Honest price tag

Reorganizing models are slower, more relational, and harder to finance than delivering models: revenue arrives late, value accrues to stakeholders who aren't payers, and the multi-logic complexity strains young teams. This is exactly why layers 3–5 exist — patient capital, multi-stakeholder ownership, and guardianship governance are the structures that let a reorganizing model survive its own gestation. Flag the coherence demand forward.
