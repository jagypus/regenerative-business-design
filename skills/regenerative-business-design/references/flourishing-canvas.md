# Layer 2 — The Flourishing Business Canvas

Source: Antony Upward / the Strongly Sustainable Business Model Group (OCAD University, flourishingbusiness.org). The FBC extends Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas from one economic layer to three nested contexts: **environment** (the biosphere everything depends on), **society** (communities, culture, institutions), and **economy** (the conventional canvas, reframed).

A business model here answers: *how does this venture co-create flourishing — and honestly, what does it destroy — across all three contexts?*

## The canvas structure

Work through four perspectives and sixteen question-blocks. In conversation, don't march through all sixteen mechanically — cluster them, but make sure each gets answered before the canvas is "done".

### Perspective 1: PROCESS (how value is created)

- **Activities** — what the business actually does, including ecological and social processes it participates in (e.g., grazing patterns, community convening), not just commercial operations.
- **Resources** — what it uses: include natural stocks (water, soil, biomass) and social resources (trust, knowledge commons) alongside capital and equipment.
- **Biophysical stocks** — the natural reservoirs the business draws on or replenishes. Name them specifically: *this* aquifer, *this* forest. Are stocks growing or depleting because this business exists?
- **Ecosystem services** — the services nature provides the business free (pollination, water filtration, climate regulation). Making these visible is half the canvas's power: they are unpriced suppliers.
- **Partnerships** — formal and informal allies, including stewardship organizations, regulators, indigenous communities, research bodies.
- **Governance** — who gets to make which decisions (preview of layer 4; on the canvas, just capture current/intended decision rights and whose interests they serve).

### Perspective 2: VALUE (what is created and destroyed)

- **Value co-creations** — value delivered *with* (not just to) each stakeholder, in all three contexts: economic value, social value (health, capability, belonging), ecological value (habitat, carbon, soil).
- **Value co-destructions** — the honest block, and the one conventional canvases lack. What does this model degrade? Extraction, emissions, displaced livelihoods, attention costs, waste. Naming destructions is a design input, not a confession — each one is a redesign target or an honest residual.

### Perspective 3: PEOPLE (who is involved)

- **Stakeholders** — all of them, including non-customers affected by the model. **Include ecological actors as stakeholders** (the river, the pollinators, future generations) — assign them a voice-holder if possible (links to layer 4 guardianship).
- **Customers (needs)** — the human needs being met (use a needs framework like Max-Neef if helpful: subsistence, protection, participation, idleness, creation...). Distinguish needs from wants the model manufactures.
- **Relationships** — the quality and kind of relationship with each stakeholder group: transactional, communal, custodial?
- **Channels** — how value reaches stakeholders, with attention to channel externalities (e.g., last-mile emissions, platform dependencies).

### Perspective 4: OUTCOMES (how success is defined)

- **Goals** — tri-profit definition of success: the firm's chosen goals for environmental, social, AND economic outcomes. Not a single bottom line with two PR appendices.
- **Benefits** — the rewards generated, in all capitals (financial, natural, social, human), and who receives them.
- **Costs** — likewise in all capitals: who and what pays, including unpriced costs to ecosystems and communities. Honest cost accounting feeds the value co-destruction block.

## Facilitation guidance

- **Start with stakeholders and value, not activities.** Founders default to describing what they do. Pull them first to who is affected and what flourishing means for each — the activities then redesign themselves.
- **Make nature concrete.** "The environment" is a level-2 abstraction. Ask: which watershed? which species? which material flows? A named ecosystem can be a stakeholder; an abstraction cannot.
- **Use the destruction block early.** Asking "what does this destroy?" mid-process, when trust is established, unlocks more honesty than saving it for the end.
- **Tri-profit goals must be specific enough to fail.** "Improve soil health" is decoration; "raise soil organic matter on partner farms from 1.8% to 3% by 2030" is a goal. Push for falsifiability in all three contexts.
- **Canvas → coherence.** When the canvas is drafted, check it against layer 1 (does the model express the essence?) and forward to layers 3–5 (does anything in the model require ownership, governance, or capital that conventional structures would corrupt?).

## Common patterns worth knowing

- **Regenerative flywheels**: models where serving one context strengthens another (e.g., agroforestry: ecological restoration *is* the product pipeline). Look for these — they are the difference between a balanced scorecard and a living system.
- **Hidden dependency inversions**: the canvas often reveals that the business's most critical supplier is an unpriced ecosystem service. That discovery motivates layer 3–4 protections (e.g., giving the watershed a governance voice).
- **Multi-stakeholder value capture conflicts**: when two stakeholders' value claims collide (cheap product vs. living wages), don't average them away on the canvas — carry the tension into governance design.
