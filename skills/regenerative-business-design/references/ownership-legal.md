# Layer 3 — Ownership & Legal Architecture

The question this layer answers: **who owns the business, who can never own it, and what locks the purpose into law rather than goodwill?** Goodwill dies with founders; structure survives them.

Jurisdiction caveat (apply everywhere in this file): availability, names, and tax treatment of these structures vary by country and change over time. Present principles + named precedents, search the web for current status in the user's jurisdiction, and always recommend local counsel for implementation.

## Steward ownership: the two principles

Steward ownership (popularized by Purpose Foundation / Purpose Stiftung) is not one legal form but a pair of binding principles, implementable many ways:

1. **Self-determination (self-governance)**: control stays with people actively connected to the business's purpose — stewards — and can never be bought, inherited, or extracted. Voting rights are held in trust for the purpose, not owned as property.
2. **Profits serve purpose**: profits are a means, not an end. They are reinvested, shared with stakeholders, or donated; capital providers get fair, capped returns. The company cannot be sold for shareholder gain ("asset lock").

Test any proposed structure against both principles. Many structures satisfy one and not the other (e.g., an ESOP can still be sold; a non-profit can have purpose-drift with no stewards).

## The structural toolkit

### Foundation ownership
A foundation holds controlling shares; operating company runs commercially.
- Precedents: **Bosch** (Robert Bosch Stiftung, since 1964), **Carlsberg**, **Novo Nordisk** (Novo Nordisk Foundation), **Zeiss** (the original, 1889), **Patagonia** (2022: voting stock → Patagonia Purpose Trust, economic stock → Holdfast Collective; "Earth is now our only shareholder").
- Strengths: extreme longevity, purpose-lock, large-scale compatible. Watch for: foundation governance ossifying; separation of stewardship from operating knowledge.

### Golden-share / veto-share model
A purpose-guardian entity (e.g., Purpose Foundation's "veto share" gGmbH in Germany) holds ~1% with veto rights over purpose changes, sale, and profit-distribution changes; stewards hold voting shares non-saleably; investors hold non-voting economic rights.
- Precedents: **Ecosia**, **Sharetribe**, **Ziel**, many Purpose-advised companies. Lightweight relative to a full foundation; works for startups.

### Perpetual purpose trust (PPT)
A non-charitable trust holds the company perpetually for a stated purpose; a trust stewardship committee exercises ownership rights.
- Precedents: **Organically Grown Company** (Oregon, 2018 — the landmark US PPT conversion), **Firebrand Bakery**, Patagonia's voting-stock trust. US-friendly (esp. states like Oregon/Delaware); increasingly used for founder exits-to-purpose.

### Employee ownership trusts (EOT) & ESOPs
Trust holds shares for employees collectively (UK EOT has statutory tax relief; US ESOP is retirement-plan-based).
- Precedents: **John Lewis Partnership**, **Riverford Organic**, **Richer Sounds**. Note: employee ownership ≠ steward ownership — an EOT company can still be sold and is purpose-locked only to employee benefit. Combine with purpose provisions if regeneration is the goal.

### Cooperatives
One-member-one-vote ownership by workers, consumers, producers, or multiple stakeholder classes.
- Precedents: **Mondragon**, **REI**, **Equal Exchange**; multi-stakeholder co-ops (Quebec solidarity co-ops) are closest in spirit to this skill. Strengths: democratic legitimacy, statutory recognition in most countries. Watch for: capital-raising constraints (addressed at layer 5), demutualization risk where law allows.

### FairShares Commons (Graham Boyd / FairShares Association)
A single company with **multiple share classes for each stakeholder type** — founders, employees/co-creators, customers/users, investors, and crucially a class representing the **commons/nature** — each with tailored voting, dividend, and wealth-accumulation rights, plus qualification rules (shares earned by contribution, not bought). Designed so the company is "incorporated as a commons": no class can capture it, and value flows track contribution.
- Boyd's Evolutesix work adds the **ergodicity rationale**: cross-stakeholder share classes pool non-diversifiable risk, raising everyone's time-average outcome; and aims at portfolios of FairShares ventures pooling with each other.
- Status: implemented via tailored articles of association (UK and several EU jurisdictions have live examples); no dedicated statute — flag this honestly. Strengths: the most complete single-vehicle expression of multi-stakeholder regeneration. Watch for: complexity; investor unfamiliarity; needs sophisticated drafting.

### Mission-flagging wrappers (weaker, often complements)
- **US Public Benefit Corporation (PBC)** / benefit corp statutes; **UK Community Interest Company (CIC)** (statutory asset lock — stronger); **B Corp certification** (a certification, not a legal form — Sanford-paradigm note: it's a level-2/3 instrument; useful signaling, no control lock).
- These flag mission but mostly do not prevent sale or capture (CIC's asset lock excepted). Treat as complements to, not substitutes for, control-layer design.

## Choosing: a decision pathway

Ask in this order:

1. **What must be locked forever?** (purpose only → golden share/PPT; purpose + profits → full steward ownership; purpose + democratic control → co-op or FairShares)
2. **Who are the natural stewards?** (founders→successors: foundation/PPT; workers: EOT/worker co-op; whole stakeholder ecosystem: FairShares Commons / multi-stakeholder co-op)
3. **What capital will it need?** (heavy external capital → need investor share classes with capped/fair returns: golden-share model, FairShares, PPT with investor notes — coordinate with layer 5)
4. **What jurisdiction?** (search current options; e.g., Germany's long-discussed steward-ownership company form (GmbH-gebV) — check current legislative status rather than assuming)
5. **What's the transition path?** (new venture: bake in at incorporation, cheap; existing company: conversion via sale-to-trust/foundation, often financed by the company's own future profits — Organically Grown's model — or by steward-ownership-aligned buyers like Purpose Ventures)

## Coherence checks with other layers

- A regenerative FBC (layer 2) whose critical stakeholder is an ecosystem ought to give that ecosystem's guardian a structural position (FairShares nature class, golden share held by an ecological trustee, or board seat — see layer 4).
- Standard VC terms (preferred stock, drag-along, liquidation preferences, exit obligation) are **structurally incompatible** with steward ownership. Don't paper over this: it determines layer 5.
- Founder economics matter and are legitimate: steward ownership ≠ founder poverty, and it doesn't even preclude a windfall — it precludes the *conventional tech-exit* windfall (selling control). A founder windfall can be structured in deliberately: capped redeemable founder shares repurchased at a formula price as cash flows allow, dividend rights that vest with tenure, phantom equity capped at a multiple, or (for conversions) seller-financed payouts from the company's own future profits — paid over years, capped at a fair multiple, and never carrying control. Design this explicitly and early; unaddressed founder economics is the most common cause of regenerative-structure failure, and a founder who feels trapped will eventually fight the lock.
